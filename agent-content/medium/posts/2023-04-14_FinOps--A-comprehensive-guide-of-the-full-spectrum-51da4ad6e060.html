FinOps: A comprehensive guide of the full spectrum

FinOps has become the buzzword of the moment in the realm of cloud cost management. While cost governance and cloud optimization is not a new concept, the evolution of FinOps has captured the attention of many.

As someone who has spent a significant amount of time running cost-focused programs for customers, my thoughts on FinOps are continuing to evolve.

One observation is that FinOps programs often aims to be ‘all-inclusive’. As such, striving to consider the full total cost of ownership (TCO) for cloud environments. Arguably, this is the best and most comprehensive way to approach cost management within the cloud. This approach is also likely to be labour-intensive and costly to execute. Unless you are spending more than $10k/month on cloud or with the intention to increase, it is also almost certainly overkill.

In this post, I delve into the diverse spectrum of cloud management concepts and examine the development of FinOps. As with most things in the cloud, there is no ‘one-size-fits-all’ solution for FinOps. My goal is to demystify various approaches used. This should also offer guidance to help you determine the best strategy for your organization.

Introducing the FinOps Spectrum

Tooling and visibility

What is it?

Start by gaining visibility into your cloud costs on your respective hyperscaler. For AWS, there are native tools like Cost Explorer. This tool will give you some insight around existing spend and potential cost optimizations. Trusted Advisor can give some high-level ideas around where services can be optimized (or removed, if unused).

There are also other third party tools such as VMWare’s Aria (previously Cloud Health) and CloudCheckr which can provide a consolidated view around spend.

Effectiveness: Low

At this end of the spectrum the focus is largely around providing visibility and not necessarily policing spend. There are cost optimization changes which can be made. However, this needs to be measured against the cost of taking your engineers away from other tasks which may be more important to the business (such as releasing features and generating revenue!).

Optimizing for spend and cleaning up resources is good from an operational hygiene point of view. Make sure you factor in the time required to get context around resource usage. This will need to come from engineers and application builders — often the most contended resource in your organisation.

I find that visibility gives insight and drives awareness around cost. This doesn’t immediately result in cost reduction in your environment. However, it does establish good operational practices with cost appreciation.

Effort: Low

Any tooling deployed should be able to at least give you a starting point as to where you should look.

This activity is more about driving visibility and understanding of cloud spend. There are likely some ‘quick-wins’ identified through these tools. Executing against this should be prioritised against other revenue-generating activities that the teams may be working on.

Cost Spend Profile: $ ($100s/month+ )

The idea being that cloud spend should be top of mind without an active FinOps function. This mean, formalised FinOps team, processes or governance attached. Spend within the cloud will remain the responsibility of the teams operating there.

What should you do now:

  • Consider how much wastage is within your cloud environment is acceptable. What does a 25–35% saving mean to your business? For example, if you’re spending $2500/month on AWS there isn’t significant value in doing ‘FinOps’
  • Decide if adding more work to your engineering team / application owners is worth the available cost-savings.
  • Educate engineers using the cloud about how they can view and understand cloud spend. This will allow empowerment and make cost conscious decisions around architecture practices.
  • Think about how you can automatically deliver cost reports and insight to consumers. This will help keep cost at the top of mind.

Cost Showback

What is it?

Allocating cost to the teams and the cost centres responsible for the spend is a good cost management practice. Amazon Cost usage reports and AWS Organization can help you with resource tagging. Resource tags allow spend to be attributed to teams to allow to understand their cloud spend.

Tools like VMWare Aria (Cloud Health) can help with building up cost perspectives on how much each team is spending within the Cloud. This is determined using resource tags so it imperative to have an effective tagging strategy.

Effectiveness: Low

To hold teams and individuals accountable for spend within the cloud you need to be able to track who is creating which resource. This is the cornerstone of any FinOps/Cost Management workstream at scale.

This item is considered low effectiveness. Whilst this will tell you who created the spend — this will not necessarily optimize cloud cost. This activity is to facilitate teams and individuals to understand their cloud spend. If it cannot be understood, then it cannot be managed.

Effort: Low/Medium

Tagging resources in principle is a easy thing to do. Yet, doing this with cloud resources already running can be time consuming. Part of this activity is tracking down who created the resource and then tagging correctly. Sometimes, this requires redeploying to make it stick. This is one of these topics which is important to get nailed sooner rather than later. The longer you leave it — the harder it is to address. This is quintessential technical debt and will grow overtime if left unchecked.

Cloud Spend: $ ($100s/month+)

Providing Cost Showback/attribution is important to get right. Irrespective of your footprint size within the cloud getting this right will make your life easier in the long run.

What should you do now:

  • Establish a tagging strategy for cost attribution
  • Build out reports to report on who is spending what — make sure that these are periodically reviewed.
  • Educate engineers and teams around how they can be cost conscious. They should know how to access these cost reports.
  • Make the individual application/owners/teams responsible for their spending within the cloud.
  • Consider targets and budgets which these teams should adhere.

Infrastructure Optimization

What is it?

Optimizing your Infrastructure by right-sizing compute, leverage reserved instances/capacity and savings plans. Consider using services like the Compute Optimizer and Trusted Advisor. Some of the real world examples of what you can do around infrastructure include:

  • Removing unused services (eg, unattached load balancers or EBS volumes)
  • Removing misconfigured resources (Deleting Elastic IP addresses)
  • Turning off unused EC2 instances
  • Deleting resources which are no longer required
  • Rightsizing Compute Instances

Effectiveness: Low/Medium

The changes which are listed here can rapidly generating savings within your cloud environment. The majority of these are considered ‘optimization’ — taking spend out after it has started occurring.

Remember to get context from the application owners and cloud engineering teams to know what each resource are using for. The effectiveness of this approach generally yields good return on investment.

Effort: Low/Medium

You will need to distract developers and application owners away from their day jobs to implement changes. The good thing, it is relatively low effort as there shouldn’t be any need to rearchitect or change the way in which the cloud is used. For the most part, this should be looking at how resources have been deployed and considering how they are being used now. If they are no longer required, then they can be turned off and even destroyed.

The effort may increase where there are abandoned resources and finding the owner is tricky! Go back and think about Cost Showback and resource tagging!

Cloud Spend Profile: $$ ($1000s/month+)

The challenge that you’re often going to find is executing against these changes. Consideration needs to be given against how much wastage there is within a cloud environment to get an idea around how much saving is there. For example, it is not uncommon to find 25–35% wastage within any cloud environment. If your bill is $5000/month and you can generate more revenue through having your engineers working on releasing new feature, then there RIO may not be there to spend significant amounts of time.

What you should think about:

  • Infrastructure optimization is one of the easier ways to quickly save money and is an early-stage activity within FinOps.
  • Every cloud environment that I’ve come across has some amount of wastage. This is typically at least in the region of 25–35% if your teams are not considering cost during design, implementation and on-going operations.
  • Tooling will give insight, but context from your engineering teams and application owners is needed to progress.
  • Remember, cloud engineers will need to do work to make savings. How does this impact their other deliverables? Saving money is good, but not if it impacts revenue generation!

Automation in situ

What is it?

Deploying automation mechanisms to ensure that service operating in a cost-effective fashion. Examples around workstreams that I have completed using automation include:

  • Tuning autoscaling tasks to ensure that spend is better aligned with usage. This can be through better scaling policies or looking at resource allocations for other compute jobs. For example, tuning Lambda functions to allocate an optimal amount of CPU/RAM. Tuning container resource allocations is another option.
  • Automating the shutdown of development or test environments outside of business hours. The AWS Instance Scheduler can be used to do this.

Effectiveness: Medium

This can be a quite an effective way to save money within your cloud environment. The activities which are listed above (non-exhaustive) are using automation to stop cloud spend when services are otherwise not in-use.

If you consider a development/test environment which is running all month (assume 730/hours). This is only being used during the 40–50 hours/week when developers at work. Shutting these down reduces the monthly usage by around 60–65% which is immediate cost efficiency.

Effort: Low/Medium

The effort required to deploy these is at the lower end of the scale. Whilst some context is required (knowing when services are in use) it doesn’t need detailed, lower-level application knowledge. A generalist cloud engineer (or even external party) could deploy the AWS Instance schedule quickly and schedule shutdown windows.

Tuning other services requires cloud knowledge, but in-depth application knowledge and application re-architecture is not required.

Cloud Spend Profile: $$ ($1000s/month+)

What you should think about:

  • These cost savings are generally modest for a low/medium level of effort. You can usually get quick RIO for the effort involved.
  • You do not need a complete FinOps practice to execute activities like this.
  • Communication to the team who use the cloud environment ahead of making these changes is key. You do not need to have application owners or key cloud engineers implementing.
  • Automation can be deployed in isolation of other on-going workstreams
  • Consider any pre-purchased capacity reservations or savings plans which negate this effort.

Third Party Consultancy

What is it?

There are a lot of companies out there who specialise in running FinOps services. My current employer Rackspace runs a lot of different cloud optimisation programs. FinOps being one of the various flavours.

The rationale behind using an external partner means that your teams can continue to focus on the building applications and revenue generating activities. FinOps can be time consuming and non-differentiation to your organisation can be run externally.

Effectiveness: Medium/High

This can be a highly effective technique. Using a third party vendor provides a unique perspective as they’ve frequently looking at bills from cloud providers. This means that they can share their learnings from doing this day in day out and provide expertise around operating in a cost-efficient way.

External providers operate in various ways:

  1. Providing expertise around what you bill should look like based on your existing use
  2. Quickly identifying where your spending is inconsistent with peers in your industry
  3. Identifying cost inefficiencies and coming up with strategies to rapidly address
  4. Running cost governance/cost optimisation programs
  5. Enabling teams to understand how best to architect with consideration for cost
  6. Directly implementing cost saving changes
  7. Consultation around advanced cost optimization techniques (outside the scope of this article).

Effort: Low

From a cloud consumer point of view the effort is relatively low depending on the engagement. Speak with a third party consultant to understand how they can assist with FinOps.

What you should think about:

  • Consultancies can operate at various levels. Think about where you are resource constrained and make sure that you’re working with a company who can meet you where you are at.
  • Consultancies can augment your team and the savings which are identified / executed against will often offset their consultancy costs.
  • Consider using a company which has a diverse range of experience across many hyperscalers. They can help with a broader knowledge base.

Cloud Spend: $$ — $$$$ (Thousands/month+)

Depending on the consultancy and the level of involvement desired.

Cost Governance

What is it?

Throughout this article we’ve been talking about cost ‘optimization’ activities. Ie, workstreams to provide visibility of what is being spent in the cloud and then look at mechanisms to optimized spend. Cost governance is more focused on the long-term lifestyle changes around cloud consumption. This intended introduce regimented processes which will create rapid results but also make them stick. Cost governance will also introduce feedback loops to aide continual improvement.

I’ve written more details article around this: Focusing just on cost optimization? You’ve already wasted money.

The conceptional idea here is to establish a cross functional team responsible for spend practices within the cloud. This may dovetail into your existing cloud centre of excellence.

I have established this and integrated this for various enterprises over the years. This is considered the forerunner to FinOps. There is substantial value of a systemic cost-governance program. however, for many organizations this can be considered overkill.

Effectiveness: Medium/High

Establishing a cost governance board (CGB) has proven effective. This reframes how the stakeholders view consumption within the cloud. Cloud architecture and cost optimization are one and the same. During the design process cloud cost can be considered as a non-functional requirement. This means that cost is discussed as part of architectural practices and solutions are devised understanding the on-going financial implications.

Effort: Medium/High

Whilst this will deliver highly optimized cloud environments there is an overhead. Establishing a cost-governance board across all cloud activities is a non-trivial activity.

Cloud Spend Profile: $$$ ($10,000s/month+)

What you should think about:

  • Do you have the necessary capabilities to establish a cost governance board?
  • Is there buy in across the business to establish a cross-functional cost governance board? Having a cloud management office or cloud centre of excellence established makes this easier to put in place.
  • Is the cloud spend sufficient enough to justify the overhead of a cost governance board?

Cloud Re-Architecture

What is it?

We often see legacy workloads being transition into the cloud which comprises of a lift and shift. This is a quick way into the cloud, but it is also generally more expensive then running this within a legacy hosting environment. Persistent running costs add up quickly.

Taking advantage of cloud native primitives will almost certainly save you money, however, they require re-architecture which is an expensive exercise.

Effectiveness: High

Taking legacy applications running in static compute instances and recrafting these to take advantage of cloud native primitives is a good cost optimization approach. You can take your billing increment from hours or minutes to seconds or milliseconds per transaction.

Effort: High

Rearchitecting applications is significant work and rarely undertaken in the real world. This is very much an activity that can be characterized as ‘spend money to save money’. In this case though — the money is engineering time and effort. This needs to be considered against other areas where you engineers could be investing time.

Cloud Spend Profile: $$$ ($10,000s/month+)

This is not for the faint-hearted. Whilst this can be accomplished and it will save money on the long-term it is a lot of commitment and will make an enormous difference.

What you should think about:

  • There is significant time required to rearchitect an application. Whilst this comes with big payoffs over the long-term there are large short-term costs associated with the work.
  • Rearchitecting comes with other benefits apart from cost optimization — disaggregating the monolith into separate microservices or using cloud native primitives allow for faster release cycles and reducing time to market.
  • This work will be distracting for the application owners. Consider how much this will slow down other development activities
  • Re-architecture does come with risk that needs to be managed
  • Remember Hofstadter’s law — “It always takes longer than you expect, even when you take into account Hofstadter’s law” Development work always takes longer than expected as the unknowns are hard to predict for.
  • There could be a short-term period when two versions of the services are running new/old. Account for the double-bubble.

Complete FinOps

What is it?

FinOps considers everything on this page and then some. The intention with FinOps is to consider every single aspect of financial operations end to end across all technology estates.

It comprises of all the above. Depending on who you speak to, FinOps is also intended to cover the wide array of governance aspects in the cloud which may touch on cost in any way, shape or form.

This quickly extends into all operational practices and operational excellence. According to some FinOps practitioners this means that it can be used to ensure that all services are operated in a consistent and governed way. This can result in it also extending across other disciplines including things like sustainability, security, reliability, performance, and architectural practices.

Effectiveness: High

This is highly effective but as it requires inputs from all other components it also requires a level of operational maturity to perform in a meaningful way.

Effort: High

This takes a level of maturity to implement and should be expected to be an extended project within its own right.

Cloud Spend: $$$ — $$$$ (Thousands to Millions/month)

What you should think about:

  • Transition to utilizing a full FinOps approach will encourage operational maturity and ensure that you are getting the best cost optimization within the cloud.
  • This is intended to transcend across the cloud practices but also look at the entire operating model (spanning people, process and technology).
  • FinOps in its entirety is incredibly broad. Unless you have substantial spend within the Cloud — you don’t need everything.
  • Third parties consultancies and cloud management businesses can help you implement a FinOps program if you do not already have the skillset in house

Conclusion

Throughout this post I have discuss various facets of FinOps within cloud environments and the various guises which it can take. There is no single ‘one-size fits all’ approach to FinOps and there are various aspects within your company to decide the best strategy.

Some of the key attributes have been discussed with this article and a summarizing table below should help guide you and your organisation around how to attack this topic.

Aspects which should be considered:

  • Operational maturity — Starting small, get tooling in place, determine how to attribute spend within your company and then continue to bolt in additional layers of governance as you mature. (Think: crawl, walk, run).
  • Cloud Spend — Review your bills and understand both your bill today, and your anticipated trajectory. If your bill is within the hundreds and will likely end up in the thousands per month then you likely only want to adopt some of the early phases. Alternatively, if that trajectory is into the millions — act quickly. Getting cost governance and control in place early will save you significantly.
  • Human Resourcing — Whilst tooling will get you part of the way, you need to focus on skills and knowledge around two areas. Firstly, around the cloud hyperscaler. You need access to knowledgeable cloud engineers and architects to be effective. Secondly, you will need access and time for the application owners and operators in the cloud to give you context around their business uses of the cloud.
