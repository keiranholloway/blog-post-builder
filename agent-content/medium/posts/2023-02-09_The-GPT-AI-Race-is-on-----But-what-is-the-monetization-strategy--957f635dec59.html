The GPT/AI Race is on! But what is the monetization strategy?

OpenAI has made a strong start, setting impressive records over the past three months. Within just five days, it achieved 1 million users, which is faster than the adoption rates of popular products like the iPhone, Spotify, and Netflix, which took 74 days, five months, and three and a half years respectively. OpenAI now attracts 13 million unique visitors per day as of January 2023.

Microsoft is investing heavily in OpenAI with a $10 billion commitment. They are already well underway with integrating ChatGPT with Microsoft Office, Teams, and their search engine, Bing. The parent company of ChatGPT, OpenAI, has seen its valuation reach $29 billion as of February 2023.

That’s some pretty impressive accomplishments over the past number of weeks.

In the second lane, we have Google launching their version, ‘Bard,’ which has been used internally for a long time and even referred to as being sentient. The initial launch of this service seems to have stumbled a bit, as Alphabet stocks lost over $100 million in market valuation.

Google’s long-term advertising revenue stream has been under threat for several quarters, with a decline of $ 100million in market valuation in Q4 2022. Search has been the backbone of the business, allowing Google to sell ads and generate capital. Generative pre-trained transformers have been touted as their saving grace

It does start to make me wonder though — what’s the long-monetization strategy?

From a IT technology vendor point of view — there’s a lot of revenue streams which are immediately obvious that could be adopted including:

  1. Consultancy: OpenAI can offer consultancy services to help organizations integrate AI into their business operations. This could likely include provision of high-performance computing resources optimised for these workstreams
  2. Data: OpenAI can sell access to its large data sets and other resources that can be used to train AI models.
  3. Research collaboration: OpenAI can collaborate with other organizations and institutions on research projects that leverage its AI technologies.
  4. Product development: OpenAI can develop AI-powered products and services for specific industries, such as healthcare, finance, or retail.
  5. Subscription services: OpenAI is already running a limited subscription service which is running out at $20/month. If multiple companies are competing in the same space — does this not just become a race to the bottom?
  6. Target AI responses — At least one of these companies has a long history with advertising revenue. Does this mean services could provide answers with biased results intended to influence purchasing decisions?
