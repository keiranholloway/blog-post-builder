Tacit Knowledge — The silient efficiency killer in your Dev/Ops teams

Technology plays a central role in shaping the trajectory of every business. Reliability, efficiency and scalabily are terms are common place in every infrastructure architecture discussion. However, even with the strategic important of technology — many organisations often struggle with day to day operational issues.

In any given operational team, and you will often find a a single, highly skilled and knowledable individual who can answer any question. These are commonly considered the ‘rockstar’ engineers are are frequently the high-performers. The underlying charaterisics though — is that they’re also the holder of significant “tacit knowledge”. They maintain the know-how in their minds which is often hard to articulate, quantify or pass-along. Unfortunately, this charaterics can also be the silent root problem of many technology operational issues.

Understanding Tacit Knowledge

It’s essential to understand what tacit knowledge is; Coined by Michael Polanyi in the 1960s, the term refers to the knowledge we possess but find hard to explicitly describe or communicate. This type of knowledge is typically acquired through personal experience or social interactions and includes skills, ideas, insights, and intuitions.

In operation teams, this can often be considered as “Tribal knowledge”

For example, a seasoned DevOps engineer intuitively knows how to solve complex system issues by simply looking at an error message and, through their implicit understanding of the context, knows the solution. This instinctual problem-solving approach is a form of tacit knowledge.

The Tacit Knowledge Dilemma in IT Operations

In DevOps, tacit knowledge poses a critical problem because it’s personal and context-specific. Here are the key ways in which it contributes to IT operational issues:

  1. Bottleneck in Knowledge Transfer: Tacit knowledge is often not documented. When experienced IT professionals leave an organization or move to other projects, their in-depth, ‘tacit’ understanding of the system often goes with them, leaving a knowledge gap. This gap can lead to operational inefficiencies, prolonged issue resolution time, and, in severe cases, system downtime.
  2. Reduced efficiency in day-to-day executation: If other team members have a fixed dependency on a single individual then the overall team output is substantially constricted.
Remember — The theory of constraints:
Maximum throughput of any process is limited by the bottleneck.
If a single person holds the knowledge to get something completed — they are a bottleneck
Any optimization outside of the bottleneck is just an illusion

3. Hindrance to Automation: As businesses continue to rely on automation for improving operational efficiency, tacit knowledge presents a roadblock. Automated systems require explicit, codified instructions. The difficulty in converting tacit knowledge to explicit rules hampers the automation of complex tasks, slowing down IT process evolution.

4. Inhibiting Innovation: Innovation thrives when knowledge is shared freely, sparking new ideas. But the unarticulated nature of tacic knowledge impedes this free flow of ideas, stalling innovation.

Mitigating the Impact of Tacit Knowledge

While it may seem daunting to tackle tacit knowledge-related issues, several strategies can help:

  1. Documentation needs to be a first-class — I maintain a mantra within the teams: “if it isn’t written down, then it hasn’t happened!” — This means that documentation is the key to everything. If you’re working on a task, then it isn’t complete until it is documented.
  2. Foster a Culture of Knowledge Sharing: Encourage employees to share their insights and experiences with the team. This could be through regular team meetings, mentorship programs, or collaborative problem-solving sessions.
  3. Invest in Knowledge Management Systems: These systems can capture, organize, and distribute knowledge across the organization. While tacit knowledge may not be easily documented, knowledge management systems can facilitate the conversion of tacit knowledge into more explicit, shareable formats. Maintain a single source of truth. Avoid duplication and everyone should know where knowledge lives. This knowledge should be democratized (which means don’t save technical documentaiton in word docs on your desktop!)
  4. Continuous Training and Upskilling: Regular training sessions can help employees gain the tacit knowledge of experienced team members. Simultaneously, upskilling programs can help to standardize the knowledge within the team, reducing reliance on individual members’ tacit knowledge.

In conclusion, tacit knowledge can no longer be the silent elephant in the room when it comes to DevOps issues. By understanding and addressing the challenges posed by tacit knowledge, organizations can significantly improve their IT operations, ensuring they remain robust, efficient, and ready to drive business success. After all, in the ever-evolving IT landscape, knowledge is not just power — it’s progress.
