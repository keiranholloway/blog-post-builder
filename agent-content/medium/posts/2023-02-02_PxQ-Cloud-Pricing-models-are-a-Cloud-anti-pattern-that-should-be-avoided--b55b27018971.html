PxQ Cloud Pricing models are a Cloud anti-pattern that should be avoided!

PxQ is a pricing model used to calculate the cost of cloud outsourcing services. This model is based on the product of the number of processing units (P) and the quantity of resources consumed (Q). For example, if you run 10 EC2 instances in AWS, you will pay $X/month for management services.

However, this model is not suitable for cloud services due to its inflexibility, one-size-fits-all approach, limited scalability, and lack of consideration for value.

  1. Inflexibility: PxQ pricing models do not account for changes in resource utilization and do not make the best use of cloud resources. For instance, it is considered a better practice to use many smaller compute instances and scale gradually according to usage, but PxQ penalizes you for doing so.
  2. One-size-fits-all approach: PxQ pricing models do not consider the unique needs and requirements of each business, making it difficult for them to choose the right resources. Furthermore, the architecture and cost are closely linked in the cloud, and PxQ can negatively influence architectural decisions.
  3. Limited scalability: PxQ pricing models limit businesses’ ability to scale resources, resulting in higher costs and inefficiencies. It also does not work well with serverless cloud primitives.
  4. Discourages the use of cloud native patterns: Using PxQ implies a legacy infrastructure support model and discounts the benefits of the latest technology. For example, serverless primitives such as FaaS, serverless container orchestration environments, or step functions, do not have compute instances to bill against.
  5. No consideration of value: PxQ pricing models do not take into account the value that businesses receive from their cloud outsourcing services, making it difficult to determine the return on investment.

In conclusion, businesses should consider alternative pricing models that better account for their unique needs and provide more visibility and control over their cloud outsourcing costs.
